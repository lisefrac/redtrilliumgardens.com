I remember distinctly when I learned the difference between “botany” and “horticulture.” It was part of a vocabulary exercise in my eighth grade English class.

It’s taken me a lot longer to *internalize* that difference, however.

“It’s all working with plants, right?” Well, plants are definitely involved in both, and there’s definitely knowledge transfer between the two topics. But:

Botany is the scientific study of plants.

Horticulture is the practice of cultivating plants.

Just because you’re good at one doesn’t mean you’re good at the other. A lot of nursery owners couldn’t tell you the scientific names of the plants they sell, and a lot of botanists couldn’t keep a houseplant alive.

And this is a lesson I keep learning again and again.

Botany is a science. I mean, physicists may scoff at it as hard science, but it is based around the scientific method. At its core, the science of botany focuses on genetics — the phylogenetic relationships between species. And what is genetics but biology? and biology boils down to chemistry, and chemistry is physics. 

Horticulture... well, there’s science to it, for sure, but I'd argue there's a large part which is an art: using uncertain symbols and cues to make something from scratch. Knowing when to sow, when to water, what a plant needs. A soil moisture gauge can't always tell you as much as a thumb in the soil can.

And like any art, there is a degree to which talent plays a part. That **plant magic** that some people seem to get for free. (Magi, I'm looking at you!)

Now, I think “talent” is hard to define, and “a lot of hard work” can look like talent. If I wanted to become a practicing visual artist, I would probably start on the back foot. But I believe I could do it, if I were sufficiently motivated and put in enough work. 

(Not that I intend to. Life is finite, etc)

Which brings me back to botany and horticulture. I think I’m good at botany. I studied cognitive science at university. I wrote an undergraduate thesis using scientific methods to study second language acquisition. I’m good at remembering words and names and random bits of trivia. Oh, and probably most importantly: I acquired my basic certificate in Native Plant Studies, which was significantly more botany than horticulture.

Horticulture, on the other hand? **I don’t think I naturally have that plant magic.**

I didn't wake up one day and know how to care for plants. And in fact, I still kill a lot of plants. Which, yes, yes, is the dirty secret of all horticulturists; it's how you learn. As I often say to my customers who tell me they have a black thumb: "the first step to being good at plants is being bad at plants."

But I still feel on the back foot here compared to other folks I know, and compared to my knowledge of botany.
